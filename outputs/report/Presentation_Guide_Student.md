## Before You Start Reading

> *"What should athletes, the city of Los Angeles, and countries around the world know before the 2028 Olympics — and how can data help them win?"*

This presentation is their final answer. It is 20 slides long and uses real Olympic data from 120 years of Games history (1896–2016) to give advice based on facts and math — not guesswork.

Here is what each slide is saying, in plain English.

## Slide 4 — The Data Foundation

Before any analysis, the team needed to understand the data they were working with. Think of this like checking your ingredients before you start cooking.

Key facts about the dataset:
- **271,116 rows** — one row for each athlete at each Olympic Games
- **135,571 unique athletes** across all those rows
- **230 countries** (called NOCs — National Olympic Committees) sent athletes
- **66 sports** and **765 events** in total
- The data covers **120 years** (1896 to 2016)

The right side lists data quality issues — things like missing weights, heights, and ages for some athletes. Good data scientists always check this before trusting their results.

## Slide 5 — Section Break: Pillar 1 — The Athlete Edge

## Slide 6 — Gender Equity: A 70-Year Transformation

The chart on this slide shows how many male vs. female athletes competed in each Olympic Games from 1948 to 2016.

**What the chart shows:**
- In 1952, only about 9 out of every 100 athletes were women
- By 2016, almost 45 out of every 100 athletes were women
- The gap has been closing steadily — and LA 2028 aims for 50/50

**Why does this matter?**
Women's sports have grown massively. More women competing means more events, more countries investing in women's programmes, and more medals up for grabs. The recommendation here is for coaches and sporting bodies to invest more in women's training — especially for the four brand new sports at LA 2028 (flag football, cricket, lacrosse, squash).

---

## Slide 7 — Home Nation Advantage: The LA Effect

This slide answers a really interesting question: **Do countries win more medals when they host the Olympics in their own country?**

The answer is yes — by a lot.

The data shows that host countries win about **22.8% more medals** than they usually do.

The most extreme example: **USA at the 1984 Los Angeles Olympics** won 83 Gold medals — more than any host country in modern history.

**Why does this happen?**
- Athletes train on familiar ground
- No jet lag or travel exhaustion
- Home crowd cheering them on boosts performance
- Local climate and venue conditions are well known

**The recommendation:** The US Olympic Committee should focus extra investment on sports where the home advantage is strongest (athletics, swimming, gymnastics, combat sports), because 2028 is America's best shot at an all-time record medal haul.

## Slide 9 — Host City Cost Benchmarking

The Olympics are expensive. But by how much?

This slide compares how much different cities spent on hosting the Olympics:

| City | Cost |
|---|---|
| LA 1984 | $0.5 Billion |
| Athens 2004 | $9 Billion |
| Beijing 2008 | **$42 Billion** |
| Tokyo 2020 | $15.4 Billion |
| **LA 2028 (projected)** | **$6.9 Billion** |

The standout story here is how **cheap** LA 2028 is compared to other recent Games. The reason is simple: **84% of the venues already exist**. They don't need to build stadiums — they already have SoFi Stadium, Crypto.com Arena, the Rose Bowl, and others.

Even better, **LA 1984 actually made a $215 million profit** — one of only a handful of Games in history to do so. The plan is to repeat that model.

## Slide 10 — LA 2028 Venue Readiness

This slide shows where the Games will actually be held. Fifteen venues are spread across Greater Los Angeles.

The key takeaway: most of these are world-class facilities that already exist and are already being used by professional sports teams. This is what makes LA 2028 different from most other host cities, which have to build expensive new infrastructure.

Examples:
- **SoFi Stadium** (NFL Rams home) → Opening/Closing Ceremonies
- **Crypto.com Arena** (NBA Lakers home) → Basketball, Boxing
- **Rose Bowl** → Football (Soccer)
- **UCLA Campus** → Olympic Village where athletes live

## Slide 12 — All-Time Olympic Medal Table

This slide answers: **which countries have won the most Olympic medals of all time?**

Unsurprisingly, the **USA is first by a huge margin** with over 5,000 total medals. The next biggest winners are Russia (which competed as "URS" — Soviet Union — for decades), Great Britain, Germany, and France.

The chart on the left shows this visually as a horizontal bar chart. The table on the right gives the exact numbers for the top 10.

One important note: some of these countries (like the Soviet Union and East Germany) no longer exist — but their medals are still counted in the all-time totals.

## Slide 13 — Olympic Medals by Continent

Instead of looking at individual countries, this slide zooms out to look at **entire continents**.

The pie chart is striking:
- **Europe** has won **59.2%** of all Olympic medals (1948–2016)
- **Americas** (mostly USA) accounts for about **21.6%**
- **Asia** has **11.2%**
- **Africa** and **Oceania** together make up just about **7.7%**

This shows how unequal Olympic success is around the world. Europe and North America have historically dominated because they had more money to invest in sports — but Asia is growing fast, especially China, Japan, and South Korea.

## Slide 14 — The Superpower Medal Race: USA vs China

This is one of the most interesting slides. It shows the **medal counts for the USA and China in every Olympics from 1984 to 2016** — plus the 2028 predictions.

**The story:**
- China didn't even compete in the Olympics until 1984
- By 2008 (hosted in Beijing, China), they nearly matched the USA
- Since then it's been a close rivalry
- In LA 2028, the model predicts USA will pull ahead again — because of the home advantage

The stars on the chart represent the 2028 forecasts. This is where data science meets geopolitics — two of the world's biggest superpowers competing not just in trade and politics, but in sport.

## Slide 16 — Model Methodology and Accuracy

This slide explains *how* the prediction model works.

Think of it like this: imagine you are trying to predict how well a student will do on a future exam. You would look at:
- How they did on the last exam
- How they've done on average over several exams
- Whether they have special advantages (like extra study time)

The Olympic model does the same thing for countries:

| What the student analogy uses | What the Olympic model uses |
|---|---|
| Last exam score | Medals won at the previous Olympics |
| Average over 3 exams | Rolling 3-Games medal average |
| Extra study time | Whether the country is the host |
| Family income | Country's GDP (how rich the country is) |
| Class size | Country's population |

Two machine learning algorithms were used (Random Forest and Gradient Boosting) and their answers were averaged together for a better result.

The model was tested by hiding one Olympics at a time and seeing how well it could predict it — this gave an average error of about **3.2 medals** per country, which is quite good.

The error bars (the little lines at the end of each bar) show the uncertainty range — the model isn't 100% certain, so it gives a low and high estimate too.

The bar chart on the right shows the same thing but grouped by continent — confirming that **Europe will still dominate overall, but Americas will get a big boost from the USA host effect**.

## Slide 19 — Strategic Recommendations

This is the action plan — one of the most important slides in the whole presentation. It is split into three columns, one for each stakeholder group.

### For Athletes and Coaches
1. Practice with crowd noise to simulate the home-crowd atmosphere
2. Start training for the new 2028 sports now (flag football, cricket, lacrosse, squash)
3. Prepare for LA's July heat — the average temperature is 27°C (81°F)
4. Stay within the ideal physical profile for your sport
5. Consider competing in two events instead of one — data shows this increases medal chances

### For the City of LA
1. Use the LA 1984 "profitable Games" story as a marketing advantage
2. Leverage Hollywood and the entertainment industry for sponsorship deals
3. Solve transportation across the 15 venues spread across the city
4. Plan post-Games use of athlete housing before the Games even start
5. Market the Games as "carbon neutral" to attract environmentally conscious sponsors

### For Countries (NOCs)
1. USA should invest in sports where home advantage is strongest
2. African countries should invest in cricket and squash — these new sports offer their best chance at Gold medals
3. Growing Asian economies (India, Indonesia, Vietnam) should invest in athletic infrastructure now
4. European countries need to defend against Asia closing the gap
5. Everyone should note that Russia and Belarus may be absent — their medals have to go somewhere

## Glossary of Terms (Words That Might Be Unfamiliar)

| Term | What it means |
|---|---|
| **NOC** | National Olympic Committee — every country's official Olympic body |
| **Pillar** | A main section or theme of the report |
| **GDP** | Gross Domestic Product — a measure of how rich a country's economy is |
| **Machine Learning** | Teaching a computer to find patterns in data and make predictions |
| **Random Forest** | A type of machine learning that builds many decision trees and averages their answers |
| **Gradient Boosting** | Another machine learning method that improves predictions step by step |
| **Ensemble** | Using multiple models together to get a better answer than any single model alone |
| **Cross-Validation** | A method for testing how accurate a model is by hiding some data and seeing if the model can predict it |
| **MAE (Mean Absolute Error)** | The average mistake size a model makes — lower is better |
| **Legacy** | What a host city is left with after the Olympics are over (stadiums, housing, infrastructure, debt) |
| **Host Advantage** | The boost in performance that a country gets when the Olympics are in their home country |
| **Benchmark** | A standard or reference point used for comparison |
| **Choropleth** | A map where areas are coloured based on a number (like medal count) |
| **Panel data** | Data that tracks the same subjects (here: countries) over many time periods |
